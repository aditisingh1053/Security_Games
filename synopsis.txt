Theme: Security Games
Classical Stackelberg Security Games assume that the defender commits to a strategy and that the attacker best responds. This breaks down in high-stakes military scenarios such as Suppression of Enemy Air Defences (SEAD), where both sides adapt simultaneously over multiple rounds under resource constraints and imperfect information.

This project models the attacker (SEAD aircraft and decoys) versus defender (surface-to-air missile systems) interaction as a repeated, simultaneous-move imperfect-information game, where the state evolves through resource depletion across stages with options for escalation or ceasefire.

We implement CFR and CFR+ on this SEAD game model, validate convergence, and study how equilibrium strategies evolve as resources deplete. We additionally treat the attacker’s decoy budget as a formal mechanism design variable, varying it to study phase transitions in strategic advantage.

As an extension, we explore modifications to the CFR framework that account for risk-averse behaviour rather than optimising purely in expectation. We investigate whether weighting outcomes by worst-case scenarios better captures how real military commanders make decisions under existential resource constraints and how such equilibria differ from their risk-neutral counterparts.

The project is implementation and experiment-oriented. We compare CFR-based Nash equilibria with a Stackelberg baseline and empirically analyse robustness across varying resource configurations, with relevance to Indian and global defence contexts.

The standard repeated SEAD model assumes the defender knows the set of possible attacker types/actions.
To better model operational uncertainty, we will extend the implementation to handle unknown attacker action sets
in the following conditions: the true attacker types are either (i) finite but initially unknown (with an upper
bound K_max or (ii) belong to a known parametric family whose parameters can be learned online. Concretely,
we propose a hybrid approach: maintain a growing catalogue of observed attacker responses and compute defender
strategies on the induced game schedule, targeted exploration rounds (inspired by online learning in Stackelberg
security games) to discover new types and produce unbiased estimators of type frequencies under partial feedback.
For the parametric variant, we perform online parameter estimation and periodically update the solver using 
the estimated attacker models. This design trades off exploration for exploitation and, under the stated 
assumptions, should allow us to obtain empirical convergence and no-regret-style behaviour comparable to 
results in the literature (Balcan et al.). We will evaluate robustness across arrival rates of novel types, feedback
regimes (full vs partial), and resource-depletion schedules, and compare against a Stackelberg baseline and CFR with 
oracle knowledge.
