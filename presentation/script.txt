================================================================================
SLIDE 3  -- Stackelberg Security Games (SSGs)
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Let's start with what a Stackelberg Security Game actually is. There are
   two players: a defender and an attacker. The defender has limited resources
   to protect a set of n potential targets.

   This is not just an abstract problem -- these games are deployed in
   real-world security settings. The US Coast Guard, the Federal Air Marshals,
   LAX airport patrols, and even wildlife anti-poaching programs use these
   models. Tambe's 2012 book gives a comprehensive overview.

   Conceptually, it's called Stackelberg because the defender commits first to
   a randomized strategy, and the attacker observes this strategy and best-
   responds to it."

[CLICK -- 'The classical assumption' box appears]

  "The classical assumption that makes the analysis simple is that there is
   a single attacker with perfectly known utility function. Under that
   assumption you can compute the optimal commitment with a linear or
   mixed-integer program."

[CLICK -- last bullet appears]

  "But in reality the defender faces many possible attacker types -- different
   poachers, different threat actors -- whose utility functions are not known
   in advance. That's where things get interesting."

DURATION: ~75 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 4  -- What the literature says about attacker uncertainty
================================================================================
SAY:
  "There are several lines of work that try to handle this uncertainty.

   First, robust optimization, by Pita and others in 2010, where you plan
   against the worst-case payoffs. This gives safe but pessimistic strategies.

   Second, Bayesian Stackelberg games -- Kiekintveld et al., Marecki et al. --
   which assume you have a prior distribution over attacker types. The downside
   is that you need to know that prior, which is usually not realistic.

   Third, learning a single unknown attacker type, by Letchford et al. and
   the 2014 paper by Blum, Haghtalab and Procaccia. They give query-efficient
   learning algorithms. But they assume only ONE unknown type, not a sequence.

   Finally, online learning against an adversarial sequence of attacker types,
   which is the 2015 Balcan, Blum, Haghtalab, Procaccia paper, our base paper."

[CLICK -- box appears]

  "We chose Balcan et al. as our starting point for three reasons. One: it
   doesn't need a prior. The adversary is allowed to pick any sequence from
   a known set K. Two: they prove sublinear regret upper bounds AND a matching
   linear-regret lower bound, so the picture is complete. And three -- this is
   the punchline -- they explicitly leave open the question, what happens when
   K itself is not known in advance? That's the question we attack."

DURATION: ~90 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 5  -- Sequential vs Simultaneous Security Games
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Before we go further, a quick word on a modeling choice we made.

   There are two natural ways to model the timing of the game. The simultaneous
   version on the left: the defender picks a coverage probability vector, the
   attacker picks a target, both at the same time, neither sees the other.
   In this model the loss function is linear in p, and standard online linear
   optimization gives root-T-log-n regret almost for free.

   On the right: the sequential, or Stackelberg, model. Here the defender
   commits FIRST, the attacker observes the committed mixed strategy, and
   then best-responds. This is what the base paper uses. The loss is no longer
   linear -- in fact it is non-linear, non-convex, and even discontinuous in p,
   because the attacker's best response can jump as p crosses a boundary.

   You might ask, 'Why pick the harder model?'"

[CLICK -- alert box appears]

  "Because sequential is strictly harder for the defender. In the simultaneous
   case the attacker has to guess, but in sequential the attacker can exploit
   any exposed corner of our coverage vector. So a regret bound proved in the
   sequential model is a worst-case bound that automatically also covers the
   simultaneous case. From a defender's safety perspective, the sequential
   model is the right place to live."

DURATION: ~90 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 6  -- Where our project sits
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Now, let me tell you exactly where our work fits. Balcan et al. give two
   results. On the upper bound side, if K -- the set of attacker types -- is
   KNOWN to the defender at the start of the game, and there are k types,
   they give an online algorithm with regret of order root of T n-squared k
   log(nk). Sublinear in T, polynomial in everything else.

   On the lower bound side, they show that if you place no restriction on K --
   if the set can have up to two-to-the-T-plus-one types -- then no online
   algorithm can avoid linear-in-T regret."

[CLICK -- 'Our question' alert]

  "So there is a gap between 'fully known finite K' and 'no restriction at all'.
   Our question is: what if K is unknown a priori, but its size is bounded by
   a known constant K-max?"

[CLICK -- last bullets]

  "This is a genuinely open middle ground. We focus on the full-information
   feedback setting -- where after each round the defender learns the identity
   of the attacker -- so that we can isolate the issue of 'unknown K' from
   the separate issue of bandit-style partial feedback."

DURATION: ~90 sec.

SECTION 2  --  PROBLEM SETUP

================================================================================
SLIDE 7  -- Game primitives (from Balcan et al., 2015)
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Now the formal model. We follow the notation of Balcan et al. exactly so
   you can map everything back to the paper.

   Top box: targets are 1 through n. The defender has R resources. A schedule
   is a subset of targets that one resource can defend simultaneously. A pure
   strategy is a valid assignment of resources to schedules. A mixed strategy
   is a distribution s over pure strategies. The matrix M is the indicator
   matrix of which targets each pure strategy covers, so the induced
   coverage probability vector p equals M times s. p_i is the probability that
   target i is defended in the resulting random draw. Capital cal-P is the
   convex polytope of all valid coverage vectors.

   Bottom box -- defender utilities. We use two functions:
     u_d^c (i)  is the defender's payoff if target i is attacked AND covered.
     u_d^u (i)  is the defender's payoff if target i is attacked AND uncovered.
   Both are bounded between minus 1 and plus 1.

   When target i is attacked under coverage p, the defender's expected utility
   is the convex combination of these two cases, weighted by the probability
   that the target is or is not covered: u_d^c(i) p_i plus u_d^u(i) times
   (1 minus p_i). This is linear in p for each fixed target i."

DURATION: ~90 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 8  -- Strategy space P for n=2
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Let me make the strategy space concrete with the simplest non-trivial case,
   n equals 2. There are two targets and one resource which is allowed to be
   idle. The coverage vector is (p_1, p_2) and the constraints are: p_1 and
   p_2 are non-negative, and their sum is at most 1.

   That gives us a 2-dimensional triangle, exactly the one that Balcan et al.
   draw in Figure 1 of their paper.

   The vertices have a clean interpretation:
     - vertex (1, 0): always cover target 1.
     - vertex (0, 1): always cover target 2.
     - vertex (0, 0): the resource is idle, no target is defended.
   Any point inside the triangle is a randomization between these three
   pure strategies. cal-P is a convex polytope."

DURATION: ~60 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 9  -- Attacker types and best response
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Now the attacker side. An attacker TYPE alpha is just a pair of utility
   functions u^c_alpha and u^u_alpha, again bounded in -1 to +1, with the same
   convention: covered versus uncovered.

   Given the defender's commitment p, type alpha computes its expected utility
   for attacking each target i, and best-responds: it picks the target that
   maximizes its own expected utility. We write this as b_alpha(p), the best-
   response function.

   The defender's per-round payoff is then u_d evaluated at the target that
   the attacker actually chose, namely b_alpha(p)."

[CLICK -- alert box appears]

  "Now here is THE key technical difficulty of the entire setting. The
   best-response operator b_alpha is piecewise constant in p. As p moves
   continuously, the attacker's best target jumps discretely from one to
   another. So the defender's loss is NOT linear, NOT convex, and NOT even
   continuous in p. That breaks all standard online learning toolboxes,
   and is why Balcan et al. need the polytope machinery we'll see in a moment."

DURATION: ~90 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 10  -- Repeated game and regret
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Putting things together. The horizon T is known. The adversary is OBLIVIOUS,
   meaning it picks the entire sequence of attacker types a_1 through a_T
   before the game starts. It does not adapt to what we play.

   Each round: we commit p_t, the attacker of type a_t plays its best response,
   the defender receives the payoff. Under full information feedback we then
   observe a_t -- the identity of the attacker -- and the first time we see
   any new type, we additionally receive its full utility function.

   The benchmark we compare against is the best fixed coverage vector in
   hindsight: the single p in cal-P that, looking back at the realized sequence
   of attackers, would have given the highest cumulative payoff."

[CLICK -- Expected regret box appears]

  "And the expected regret is exactly what you'd expect: the cumulative payoff
   of that best fixed strategy minus the expected cumulative payoff of our
   online algorithm. The expectation is only over the algorithm's internal
   randomness, not over the adversary, because the adversary is fixed."

DURATION: ~90 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 11  -- Balcan, Blum, Haghtalab, Procaccia (2015) -- known K
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Here is the result we are extending -- Theorem 5.1 of Balcan et al."

[Box appears]

  "If K is KNOWN at the start, with k attacker types, and feedback is full
   information, there is an online algorithm achieving expected regret of
   order root of T n-squared k log(nk). The key features:
   sublinear in T -- so per-round regret tends to zero;
   polynomial in n and in k.
   This is what makes 'no-regret learning in Stackelberg security games'
   feasible at all."

[CLICK -- bullets appear]

  "The proof technique is what we will inherit. They partition the strategy
   space cal-P into finitely many best-response regions, enumerate the
   epsilon-approximate extreme points of those regions, and run an online
   learning subroutine over those extreme points as an expert set.

   They also prove a matching lower bound: if K is unrestricted -- if it
   could have up to two-to-the-T-plus-one types -- then linear regret is
   forced. So SOME restriction on K is necessary."

[CLICK -- alert appears]

  "And our extension, in one sentence: K is unknown a priori, but bounded by
   a known constant K-max. The first time a type appears, we get to observe
   its full utility function -- consistent with the full-information setting."

DURATION: ~90 sec.

SECTION 3  --  PROOF, ALGORITHM, AND RESULTS  (the heart of the talk)
================================================================================

================================================================================
SLIDE 12  -- Primer: half-spaces and convex polytopes
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Before we get into the proof, a quick primer on the geometric objects we
   need. Anyone who's seen LP duality will be familiar.

   A HALF-SPACE in R^n is the set of points satisfying a single linear
   inequality, a-transpose-p less than or equal to b. It is the half of R^n
   on one side of the hyperplane.

   A CONVEX POLYTOPE is a bounded intersection of finitely many such
   half-spaces.

   Now the security-game-specific definitions. For each attacker type alpha
   and each target i, we define cal-P-i-alpha as the set of coverage vectors
   for which alpha's best response is exactly target i. This is a convex
   polytope, because saying 'alpha attacks i' is exactly the conjunction of
   the linear inequalities U_alpha(i, p) is at least U_alpha(j, p) for every
   other target j.

   And then for a 'response profile' sigma -- a function from a catalogue of
   types C to N that says which target each type would attack -- the region
   cal-P-sigma(C) is the intersection over all alpha in C of those polytopes.
   It's again a convex polytope. These polytopes will be the building blocks
   of the algorithm."

DURATION: ~90 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 13  -- A concrete example for n=2: one best-response cut
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Let me make this concrete in our 2-target example so the picture sticks.

   On the left you see the triangle cal-P. We have ONE attacker type alpha_1.
   The boundary of its best-response regions is the SINGLE line where
   U_alpha_1 of target 1 equals U_alpha_1 of target 2. That line cuts the
   triangle into two polygons.

   On one side -- the green region in the picture -- alpha_1 prefers target 1.
   On the other side -- the orange region -- alpha_1 prefers target 2. So
   cal-P is split into 2 convex polytopes. That's how a single attacker
   refines the strategy space."

DURATION: ~60 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 14  -- Adding more types refines the partition
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Now what happens if we add more attacker types. Top picture: catalogue of
   2 types, alpha_1 and alpha_2. Each type contributes its own boundary line.
   The first split has been REFINED -- the two original regions are each cut
   further by the second line. We now get a finer partition into convex
   polygons.

   Bottom: with 3 types we have 3 lines, and the partition is even finer.
   The general rule: adding type alpha introduces n-choose-2 boundary
   inequalities -- one for each pair of targets. For n equal to 2 that is
   exactly one line per type, which is what we see.

   Two important observations on the right:

   First, existing polytopes get SLICED, never lost. The partition only ever
   gets finer when a new type arrives.

   Second, and this is critical -- inside any single region cal-P-sigma(C),
   the best response of every catalogued attacker is FIXED. So the defender's
   payoff, as a function of p inside that region, is LINEAR. The non-linearity
   only happens AT region boundaries, where the best response jumps. Inside
   any region we're back in linear-algebra land."

DURATION: ~100 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 15  -- The E(C) set of extreme points
================================================================================
SAY:
  "OK, here is the key object that powers the entire analysis: the set
   cal-E of epsilon-approximate extreme points of the partition.

   The point of the previous slide was that the defender's payoff is linear
   inside each polytope cal-P-sigma. A standard fact from linear programming
   is that a linear function on a convex polytope attains its maximum at a
   VERTEX. So if we want to find the best fixed coverage vector across the
   whole strategy space, we know it can only happen at a vertex of one of
   these regions.

   So we define cal-E of C, with parameter epsilon, as the set of
   epsilon-approximate vertices of all the regions cal-P-sigma. Think of it
   as a finite set of candidate strategies, one per regional vertex.

   Two key facts from the base paper:

   First, the upper-most equation is Lemma 4.3. It says that the best element
   of cal-E does almost as well as the best fixed strategy in all of cal-P,
   up to an additive error of 2 epsilon T. So restricting our search to the
   finite set cal-E only loses 2 epsilon T in regret -- a price we will absorb.

   Second, the bottom equation is Lemma 4.4. It bounds the SIZE of cal-E.
   For a catalogue of size |C|, the cardinality of cal-E is at most order of
   (2 to the n plus C n squared) to the power n, times n to the power C.
   Polynomial in C for any fixed n. That's why we can afford to run an
   experts algorithm over cal-E."

DURATION: ~110 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 16  -- E(C) grows as new types are discovered
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Visually: as the catalogue of discovered types grows, cal-E grows with it.

   Left panel: |C| equals 1, the partition has 2 regions, and there are 4
   vertices marked in red. That is cal-E for one type.

   Middle: |C| equals 2, the partition has more regions, more vertices --
   cal-E now has 6 elements.

   Right: |C| equals 3, the partition is finer still, and cal-E has 9
   vertices.

   Two things to note. First, |cal-E of C_i| is uniformly bounded by what we
   call N-max -- the value of |cal-E| evaluated at the maximum allowed
   catalogue size K-max. So even though cal-E is dynamic, its size never
   exceeds N-max. Second, and this is what makes the algorithm work: at any
   point in time, the analysis only uses the CURRENT cal-E, not future ones.
   We do not need to anticipate which types will arrive."

DURATION: ~80 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 17  -- Our main theorem
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Now we are ready to state our main result."

[Box]

  "Let K be an unknown set of attacker types whose size is bounded by K-max.
   Under full information feedback, against any oblivious adversarial
   sequence of length T, our algorithm -- which we'll see in a moment, called
   GROW-CATALOGUE -- achieves expected regret of order root of T times K-max
   times the quantity n-squared plus K-max log n.

   Three remarks on this bound.

   First, it has the SAME functional form as Balcan et al. Theorem 5.1, with
   their k literally replaced by our K-max. So we pay nothing extra in the
   exponents -- just the gentle replacement of the known k with the worst-case
   bound.

   Second, when K-max is a constant, this becomes order n root T, which
   matches the known-K rate up to logarithmic factors. So at constant K-max
   the cost of NOT knowing K up front is essentially zero.

   Third, the bound is instance-adaptive: if only K-prime distinct types
   actually appear in the realized sequence, the bound automatically tightens
   to root of T K-prime times the same parenthesized factor. So if the
   adversary is mild, we pay less."

DURATION: ~110 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 18  -- Proof idea in one picture
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Here is the proof in one picture. Look at the time-line. The horizon
   1 to T is split into FIVE intervals -- I'll call them epochs.

   The red marks tau_1, tau_2, tau_3, tau_4 are the rounds at which a new
   attacker type is OBSERVED for the first time. Between two consecutive
   such rounds, the catalogue is fixed. Inside epoch i, the catalogue C_i
   has exactly i types.

   The proof is then five steps.

   Step 1: split the horizon into epochs as just described.
   Step 2: within each epoch, the catalogue is fixed, so we can run an
   online learning subroutine on cal-E of C_i.
   Step 3: apply Balcan et al.'s within-epoch analysis -- which combines a
   regret bound for the subroutine with Lemma 4.3 -- to each epoch.
   Step 4: sum over the at most K-max epochs using Cauchy--Schwarz.
   Step 5: at most K-max rounds are 'discovery rounds' where we played
   blind because the attacker was new. Each such round contributes at most
   2 to the regret, so the discovery rounds contribute a 2 K-max additive
   constant.

   Five steps. Let me go through the analytic pieces in detail."

DURATION: ~100 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 19  -- Proof -- within-epoch regret
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Step one of the analytic part: the WITHIN-EPOCH regret.

   The first box is what Balcan et al. call Proposition 1. It says: for any
   fixed expert set cal-E_i, and any number of rounds L_i within an epoch,
   there is an online learning algorithm whose expected regret is bounded by
   c_1 times root of L_i times log of the size of cal-E_i. This is the
   standard Hedge / Multiplicative Weights guarantee adapted to losses in
   minus-1 plus-1.

   Why does this matter? Because within an epoch, the catalogue is fixed,
   and the loss is well-defined on each element of cal-E_i. So we can run
   a black-box experts algorithm and get root-L_i regret against the BEST
   element of cal-E_i for that epoch."

DURATION: ~75 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 20  -- Proof -- still within-epoch (Lemma 4.3)
================================================================================
SAY:
  "But we want to compete with p-star, the best fixed strategy in ALL of
   cal-P, not just the best element of the finite set cal-E_i. That's where
   Balcan et al.'s Lemma 4.3 comes in.

   Inside epoch i, the sequence of attackers only uses types from C_i.
   Lemma 4.3 then says: the best element of cal-E_i is almost as good as
   the best p in the whole continuous polytope cal-P, up to an additive
   error of 2 epsilon L_i.

   So combining the two boxes, I have, on one hand, an algorithm that does
   almost as well as the best in cal-E_i, and on the other hand the best
   in cal-E_i is almost as good as the best in cal-P -- including p-star.
   Chain these and you get a regret bound for the algorithm against
   p-star, restricted to epoch i."

DURATION: ~75 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 21  -- Proof -- combining the two within an epoch
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Here is the chained inequality, written out. For every epoch i, the
   expected utility of the algorithm restricted to that epoch is at least:
   the cumulative utility of p-star restricted to that epoch,
   minus a 2 epsilon L_i term -- the Lemma 4.3 approximation loss,
   minus a c_1 root of L_i log of cal-E_i term -- the within-epoch regret
   of the online learning subroutine.

   Read it as: 'algorithm payoff is within (a small approximation gap) plus
   (a small algorithmic regret) of the benchmark, on each epoch separately.'
   This is the per-epoch building block."

DURATION: ~60 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 22  -- Proof -- summing epochs with Cauchy--Schwarz
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Now we sum the per-epoch inequality over all epochs. Three contributions
   pop out.

   First, two-K-max for the discovery rounds we agreed to absorb.
   Second, two epsilon T for the total Lemma-4.3 approximation losses --
   note that the epoch-wise 2 epsilon L_i terms summed to 2 epsilon T because
   L_i sum to T.
   Third, c_1 times root of log N-max -- which is the LOG of the maximum
   cal-E size -- multiplied by the SUM over epochs of root L_i."

[CLICK -- Cauchy-Schwarz line appears]

  "Now apply Cauchy--Schwarz to that sum. The sum of root L_i over at most
   K-prime epochs is at most root of K-prime times the sum of L_i, which is
   in turn at most root of K-max T. That's the only place K-max enters
   multiplicatively in the leading term."

[CLICK -- final bound]

  "Plug in epsilon equal to one over root T -- a fixed choice -- and use
   the bound on log N-max from Lemma 4.4, which gives n-squared plus
   K-max log n. The result is exactly our main theorem:
   regret of order root of T K-max times the quantity n-squared plus
   K-max log n. Q E D."

DURATION: ~105 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 23  -- Regime in which the bound is meaningful
================================================================================
SAY:
  "One useful sanity check: when does this bound ACTUALLY say something
   meaningful, that is, when is it sublinear in T?

   The bound is sublinear iff K-max times the parenthesized factor is
   little-oh of T. Equivalently, K-max should be little-oh of the minimum of
   two quantities: T over n-squared, and root of T over log n.

   In words:
     If K-max is constant -- order one -- the bound is order n root T,
     which is great for any n up to root T.
     If K-max scales as some polynomial in n, say n-to-the-c, the bound is
     sublinear as long as T grows faster than n to the c-plus-2.
     If K-max grows roughly as T over n-squared, the upper bound becomes
     trivial -- and that's not a weakness of our analysis, it's matched by
     the linear-regret lower bound of Balcan et al. on the unrestricted side.

   So we cleanly cover the regime that the lower bound leaves open."

DURATION: ~90 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 24  -- The GROW-CATALOGUE algorithm
================================================================================
SAY:
  "OK -- with the analysis behind us, here is the algorithm itself.

   We initialize an empty catalogue C, an empty expert set cal-E, and we set
   the approximation parameter epsilon to one over root T.

   For each round t from 1 to T:
     If cal-E is still empty -- that is, we haven't seen any attacker yet --
     play a default uniform coverage. Otherwise, sample p_t from the
     current Hedge distribution q_t over cal-E.

     Then observe the attacker a_t.

     If a_t is a NEW type -- not in C -- this is a discovery round. We add
     a_t and its utility function to C, recompute cal-E from scratch on the
     enlarged catalogue, and restart Hedge on the new expert set.

     Otherwise, this is a routine round. We compute the loss l_t at every
     extreme point in cal-E, and update Hedge using an anytime learning rate
     eta of t-local equals root of log cal-E over t-local. This anytime
     schedule is what gives us the root-L_i per-epoch regret without having
     to know the epoch length in advance.

   We implement two variants of step 'recompute cal-E':
     'Total recomputation' -- rebuild from scratch every time. Easier to
     verify.
     'Incremental' -- only update the new vertices and reuse the old Hedge
     weights for the surviving extreme points. Same regret guarantee, just
     faster wall-clock."

DURATION: ~120 sec.


================================================================================
SLIDE 25  -- Experiment design
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Now to validate the theorem empirically. We need an adversary that
   actually exercises the catalogue-growing behaviour, but without being so
   adversarial that the experiment becomes pathological.

   We use what we call a 'staggered-uniform' adversary. We split the horizon
   into K-max phases of equal length. In phase i, the attacker is drawn
   uniformly at random from the FIRST i types alpha_1 through alpha_i.

   So in phase 1 only alpha_1 attacks. In phase 2, both alpha_1 and alpha_2
   are present and each appears with probability one-half. In phase i, all i
   revealed types are equally likely.

   Concretely: n equals 3, K-max equals 4, T equals 4000, and we average
   over 40 random instances of the game's utility functions.

   Two things to note about this design. First, OLD types keep arriving
   after they've been revealed -- we don't forget alpha_1 once alpha_2
   appears. That makes the benchmark p-star a genuine compromise across
   types, not a single specialist. Second, the reveal rounds are at exactly
   0, 1000, 2000, and 3000 -- so we have a clean signal of exactly when

SLIDE 28  -- Take-aways
================================================================================
SAY:
  "Three quick take-aways before I wrap up.

   First: in repeated security games we deliberately analyze the SEQUENTIAL
   Stackelberg model because it is strictly harder than the simultaneous
   one for the defender. A bound there is a worst-case bound that covers
   the easier setting too.

   Second: when K is unknown but bounded by a known constant K-max, we
   prove a regret bound of root of T K-max times n-squared plus K-max log n.
   That is the same functional form as Balcan et al.'s Theorem 5.1 with
   their k replaced by our K-max. The cost of not knowing K up front is
   essentially absorbed into K-max.

   Third: the bound is meaningful as long as K-max times that parenthesized
   factor is much less than T -- and outside that regime the matching
   linear-regret lower bound of Balcan et al. takes over. So our result
   sits cleanly in the gap that the original paper leaves open.

   Fourth, the experiments confirm everything. Sublinear regret, visible
   spikes at discovery rounds, decay to zero between."

================================================================================
LIKELY QUESTIONS AND ONE-LINE ANSWERS
================================================================================

Q1. "Why is sequential strictly harder than simultaneous from the
     defender's perspective?"
A1. Because in sequential Stackelberg the attacker observes the defender's
    mixed strategy and best-responds optimally to it -- so any randomization
    is exposed. In simultaneous, the attacker has to guess, which limits
    what it can exploit. Hence the defender's worst-case loss is higher in
    sequential. A bound proved there is automatically valid for the easier
    simultaneous case.

Q2. "What if K_max is not known either?"
A2. Then we are in the regime of Balcan et al.'s lower bound: with no bound
    at all on K, every online algorithm has linear-in-T regret. So K_max
    being known is the minimal extra information we need. In practice, K_max
    is set by domain knowledge -- 'no more than 5 distinct threat actors'
    -- and the algorithm is robust to over-estimation: if we set K_max
    larger than the actual K, the bound just degrades polynomially in K_max.

Q3. "What is the role of the epsilon parameter?"
A3. epsilon trades off the discretization error of cal-E (which is 2 epsilon T)
    against the size of cal-E (which grows polynomially in 1/epsilon).
    Setting epsilon equal to 1/root(T) balances the two and gives the
    sublinear regret rate.

Q4. "Why Cauchy-Schwarz at the end?"
A4. We have a per-epoch regret of root(L_i log N_max) for each of K' epochs.
    The sum of root(L_i) over at most K' epochs is, by Cauchy-Schwarz, at
    most root(K' * sum of L_i) = root(K' T). That's how K_max enters the
    final bound.

Q5. "How does this compare to the original Balcan et al. bound?"
A5. The functional form is identical: root(T n^2 K_max log(n K_max)) versus
    their root(T n^2 k log(nk)). The only difference is that they have
    the actual k whereas we have the upper bound K_max. When the realized
    number of types K' is less than K_max, our bound is also instance-
    adaptive in K' for the leading term.

Q6. "Why does the per-round regret in your plot go negative or near-zero
     between discovery rounds?"
A6. Because Hedge converges quickly inside each epoch -- after about
    50-100 rounds it has identified the best expert in the current
    catalogue. The benchmark p* is a single FIXED strategy that has to
    work across the whole sequence, so our adaptive play can match or
    even beat it within a single epoch. The negative regret is not a
    contradiction -- the theorem is an UPPER bound, not a lower bound.

Q7. "What changes if you allow an adaptive adversary?"
A7. In the full-information setting, even the adaptive case is handled by
    the same proof -- Hedge's regret guarantee is robust to adaptive
    adversaries. In the partial-information setting it is genuinely open;
    that's one of our future directions.

Q8. "Is the algorithm computationally efficient?"
A8. Each Hedge update is order |cal-E|. Recomputing cal-E from scratch
    when a new type is discovered is exponential in n in the worst case,
    but for fixed n and K_max it is a constant. The incremental variant
    avoids the recomputation by reusing weights for surviving vertices.

Q9. "How does this scale to large n?"
A9. The bound has an n^2 term, so it grows polynomially in n. The bottleneck
    in practice is the size of cal-E, which is exponential in n in the
    worst case. For large n one would need approximation schemes for
    cal-E or different algorithmic ideas; our experiments use n = 3 where
    everything is tractable.

Q10. "What is the practical value of full-information feedback?"
A10. It models settings where a successful attack is logged with the
     attacker's identity -- e.g. cyber attacks where forensics reveal the
     threat actor. The partial-information version, where you only see
     which target was attacked, is the natural next step and is what
     Section 6 of Balcan et al. handles for the known-K case.
