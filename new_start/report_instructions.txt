\begin{abstract}

This says the paper/report in a nutshell. This should be short, yet should provide all the jobs done in the paper.

\end{abstract }

\section{Introduction}

This is a little elaborate (but not that much where it becomes a storybook) introduction to the problem. Particularly, including (but not limited to) (a) what problem is being addressed, (b) how that problem is useful (examples from the real world will be useful here), (c) how the methods/results of your paper are different from that of the rest.

\subsection{Contributions}

This subsection is often used to provide a bulleted list (\begin{itemize } \item \end{itemize}) of major works/contributions made in the paper. This is very useful to assess the tasks performed in scientific works.

\subsection{Related Work}

This section gives the credit to all the earlier works in the domain where the current problem is generated from. This section also identifies and positions the work, i.e., shows the difference, in the perspective of the available knowledge of the domain.

\section{Model}

The heart of the paper. This introduces every notation used and provides the definition used in the paper. If the paper is very technical, a brief "preliminaries/background" subsection can also be presented to familiarize an uninitiated reader with this problem.

\section{Main Results}

If "model" is the heart, this section is the blood. Using the notation and definitions of the model, this section provides the main results. This may not just be one section, rather each different result, based on their volume and type of content may be divided into multiple sections, and often in subsections of the section. For papers with mostly experimental work, these sections serve the same purpose of elaborating the reason to reach the conclusions of the work/project. A paper may have both theory and experimental components and they can be written in different sections with the same objective in mind: guiding the reader on why we should reach the conclusion of the paper.

\section{Conclusions}

This is the formal end of the paper. This summarizes all the results in brief and comments on where the results of the paper/report were different from the known results.
